Tax-Advantaged Accounts

It is an account with tax-saving benefits. You put money in pre-tax, and don't have to pay taxes on the money coming out either. Examples of a tax-advantaged account are the Health Savings Account (HSA) or the Flexible Spending Account (FSA).

HSA & FSA advantages

Tax Advantages

Since contributions are not taxed, you can reduce your taxable income by the amount you contribute to your account. You can then use those pretax dollars to pay for health care expenses that would have otherwise been paid with after-tax dollars.

Mulitple uses

Depending upon which account you have, you may be able to pay for eligible medical, dental, vision, and prescription drug expenses. Funds may even be used for eligible expenses for your spouse or eligible dependents. See the list of eligible expenses on www.wexinc.com.

Easy to Access

Funds in the account are easily accessed with the health care payment card. Simply use it as you would a debit card. Your account balance is available at any time online, over the phone, or on the mobile app.

Health Savings account

A Health Savings Account (HSA) is like a 401(k) for health care.

An HSA is available if you enroll in the Healthy Savings Plan Medical Option. HSAs are tax-advantaged accounts that accumulate interest and can earn investment returns. The funds can be used to pay for qualified medical expenses today or can be saved for future expenses. Contributions will begin on the first earned paycheck that includes the first of the month following eligibility for the health care plans.

Advantages of a Health Savings Account (HSA)

  • Triple tax savings
  • Interest/investments
  • Portable: Yours to keep
  • Save for retirement
  • Change contribution anytime
  • Contribution limits:
    • Self: $3,850
    • Family: $7,750
    • Catch Up: $1,000
  • 100% Rollover

HSA Eligibility

The IRS requires you to be enrolled in a qualified high deductible health care plan to be eligible to contribute to a health savings account. There are still situations that may make you ineligible regardless of being enrolled in a qualifying health care plan.

Some examples include:

  • Your care is covered by your own or your spouse’s non-HSA-qualified health plan (Traditional Plan Option, Tri-Care, Medicare), Flexible Spending Account (FSA), or Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA).
  • You can be claimed as a tax dependent on another person’s taxes. Generally, this excludes children under age 19 (or age 24 for students), or other household members with limited income who are supported by the taxpayer.

Tax advantaged accounts

Health Care Flexible Spending Account

You can make contributions to the health care flexible spending account if you are enrolled in the Traditional Plan or waived healthcare.

  • The contribution limit for the health care flexible spending account (FSA) for 2023 is $2,850.
  • You can rollover up to $570 of any unused funds from your 2022 FSA.
  • As a reminder, you must elect a contribution amount for 2023 to have funds rollover.
  • The deadline to complete 2022 claims is March 1, 2023. Any balances over the rollover limit will be forfeited.

Dependant Care Flexible Spending Account

The contribution limit for the dependent care flexible spending account is $5,000. This account does not allow for a rollover of any funds. The deadline to complete 2022 claims is March 1, 2023. All remaining balances will be forfeited.

Limited Purpose Flexible Spending Account

You can make contributions to the limited purpose flexible spending account (LPFSA) if you are enrolled in the Healthy Savings Plan only. This account is for dental and vision expenses only.

  • The contribution limit for 2023 is $2,850.
  • You can rollover up to $570 of any unused funds from your 2022 FSA.
  • As a reminder, you must elect a contribution amount for 2023 to have funds rollover.
  • The deadline to complete 2022 claims is March 1, 2023. Any balances over the rollover limit will be forfeited.

When does it make sense to contribute to the LPFSA?

  • When you’re making the maximum contribution to the HSA
  • Allows you to budget for dental and vision expenses without using your HSA
  • You have healthcare FSA funds (or anticipate having) at the end of 2022
  • By electing the LPFSA, remaining funds in your 2022 healthcare FSA would rollover into the 2023 LPFSA
